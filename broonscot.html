Broonscots

Scotland

The Beginning

Documentary and travel photographer, Hermann Rodrigues, has recorded community history, new immigration, religions, professions, activities, historical buildings - and the Sikh Scottish tartan. His collection of  colour photographs highlight the diversity of India and Indian immigrant lifestyles and culture in Scotland. Rodrigues dislikes the term ‘Asian’ because it is restrictive in his mind as it “covers part of a continent almost four thousand miles across”.

Rodrigues is a photographer with a deep passion for the camera and the Asian community in Scotland. Hermann sees his work as having two interlocking purposes: firstly to express the ‘colour and vibrancy of the Asian community’ and secondly to highlight how they have imbibed themselves in Scottish culture. The photographs give a glimpse of these cross-connections and are a celebration of this diversity.

Hermann has travelled the length and breadth of India and this selection shows the diversity of the country juxtaposed with some images of South Asians in Scotland.

Hermann’s photographs have even brought out some surprising facts. Did you know, for instance, that the Leith area of Edinburgh has its own tartan? Or that Madras College in St Andrews is named after the educational system of the Indian region? Did you know that there is a town named Patna in South Ayrshire named after the capital of Bihar State in India?

Rodrigues continues to collect stories highlighting and celebrating the diversity of the South Asian community of Scotland. He calls himself “A social anthropologist with a Nikon”

Hermann Rodrigues’ collection stretches across Scotland and documents over 30 years of the South Asian community. His Broon Scots Project gives a glimpse of the outstanding collection that presents the complexity of identity, migration and assimilation of the South Asian community in Scotland. This project is his lifetime’s labour of love.
